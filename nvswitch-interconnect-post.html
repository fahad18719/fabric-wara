The Fabric Wars:
NVSwitch, NVLink,
UALink & Ultra Ethernet

Inside the switching silicon that makes 72-GPU racks think as one — and the open-standard coalition trying to break NVIDIA's grip on the scale-up fabric.

By Fahad Najam  ·  June 2026  ·  © 2026 Fahad Najam Consulting

Every time a transformer layer synchronizes gradients across hundreds of GPUs, two things happen invisibly fast: the compute you planned for — and the data movement you didn't budget for. The fabric connecting those GPUs either multiplies your throughput or becomes the ceiling you can't break through. This is the story of that fabric.

"NVIDIA didn't just sell better GPUs. They sold a switching architecture that made every GPU feel like local memory. That's the moat."

Part I — NVSwitch: The Crossbar That Changed Everything

What NVSwitch Actually Is

Most engineers know NVLink as the fast lane between two GPUs. Fewer understand that NVLink alone doesn't scale — it's a point-to-point protocol. Connecting 8 GPUs in a full mesh without a switch would require 7 direct links per GPU. That's physically unworkable. NVSwitch solves this by inserting a dedicated crossbar switching ASIC between every GPU, making the topology a software abstraction rather than a physical wiring constraint.

The Crossbar Architecture — How It Works

The internal architecture of NVSwitch is deceptively simple at the concept level: an N×N fully-connected crossbar, where every input port is hardwired to every output port through a switching matrix. The guarantee is non-blocking — any two ports can transfer simultaneously without stealing bandwidth from any other pair.

Part II — NVLink: The Protocol Layer

NVLink is the physical + protocol layer. NVSwitch is the ASIC that implements it at scale. Think of NVLink as the language and NVSwitch as the postal system that routes it efficiently. Without NVSwitch, NVLink is a fast road between two cities. With NVSwitch, it becomes a national highway grid.

Key Architectural Properties of NVLink

Beyond raw bandwidth, what makes NVLink architecturally significant is cache coherence and unified memory semantics. A GPU on one end of an NVLink fabric can directly load from another GPU's HBM using the same load/store instructions it would use for local memory. For all-reduce and all-gather collectives — the bread and butter of distributed training — this is the difference between microseconds and milliseconds.

The Architectural Insight

NVSwitch doesn't just move data faster. It preserves the NUMA memory model across 72 GPUs — making 13.5 TB of HBM in one GB200 NVL72 rack feel like one contiguous address space to the CUDA runtime. That is what NVLink + NVSwitch together create: a scale-up domain with load/store semantics, not a network.

Part III — UALink: The Open Standard Challenge

In May 2024, a coalition of companies announced they were done waiting for NVIDIA to open its interconnect. The result: UALink — Ultra Accelerator Link — an open standard designed to do for GPU-to-GPU interconnect what Ethernet did for networking: commoditize the layer, expand the ecosystem, and eliminate single-vendor dependency.

Founding Members

AMD, Broadcom, Cisco, Google, HPE, Intel, Meta, Microsoft — later joined by AWS, Apple, Alibaba Cloud, Synopsys, and 70+ organizations total. This is not a standards committee of also-rans. These are the hyperscalers and chip vendors that collectively decide what gets built next.

The first commercial UALink hardware is expected from AMD, Intel, and Astera Labs in late 2026. AMD's Helios rack systems will tunnel UALink over conventional Ethernet switches initially — running the protocol while silicon partners finalize native UALink ASICs.

Part IV — Ultra Ethernet: Scale-Out, Not Scale-Up

Here's where people get confused: Ultra Ethernet is not a replacement for NVLink or UALink. It solves a different problem at a different layer.

The Two-Fabric Model

Scale-Up Fabric (within a rack or pod): NVLink/NVSwitch or UALink. High bandwidth, low latency, load/store memory semantics. This is where all-reduce happens.

Scale-Out Fabric (rack-to-rack, datacenter-to-datacenter): InfiniBand today, Ultra Ethernet tomorrow. Message-passing semantics. This is where gradient synchronization happens across nodes.

Ultra Ethernet Consortium — backed by many of the same players as UALink — is building enhanced Ethernet that competes with InfiniBand's low-latency, congestion-controlled capabilities for HPC and AI. Broadcom's Tomahawk Ultra ASIC (51.2 Tbps, nearly 2× NVLink switch bandwidth) represents their bet that Ethernet can do both jobs: scale-up and scale-out, eliminating the need for two separate fabric technologies.

Part VII — Strategic Takeaway

The three technologies are not in a three-way race for the same prize. Each lives at a different layer of the data movement stack — and understanding that layering is what separates infrastructure architects from infrastructure operators.

NVIDIA built a vertical stack from silicon to software, and the NVSwitch crossbar is the keystone that makes it structurally sound. Every competitor's GPU is bounded at 8-GPU scale until they have an equivalent scale-up switching fabric. UALink is the industry's coordinated bet that they can build that equivalent — open, multi-vendor, at a fraction of the lock-in cost. Ultra Ethernet is the parallel bet that the scale-out problem (rack to rack) also belongs on open standards.

What makes 2026–2027 the inflection point: for the first time, the open standard will have shipping silicon. That's when the real comparison begins. Not spec sheets against production hardware — but production against production.

"NVIDIA's advantage isn't just bandwidth. It's that NVSwitch makes 72 GPUs share memory like one machine. UALink's 1,024-accelerator ceiling looks impressive — until you ask whether the latency and coherence model hold at that scale."

The Question That Matters in 2026

Can UALink deliver load/store memory coherence at 1,024-accelerator scale with sub-150ns switch latency — consistently, in production, across multiple vendors' silicon — while NVIDIA ships NVLink 6.0 on Rubin with 260 TB/s rack-scale bandwidth? That answer will determine the interconnect landscape for the rest of the decade.
